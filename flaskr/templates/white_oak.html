White Oak

History

White Oak (Quercus alba) is a type of oak tree that is native to the eastern United States. It has been an important part of the region's history and culture for centuries, and has been used for everything from shipbuilding to furniture making. Today, White Oak is a popular ornamental tree and can be found in parks, gardens, and other landscaped areas throughout the United States.

Biology

White Oak is a large deciduous tree that typically grows to a height of 60 to 100 feet. It has a broad, spreading canopy and deeply lobed, green leaves that turn reddish-brown in the fall. White Oak produces acorns that are an important food source for many animals, including deer, squirrels, and birds.

Fun Facts

White Oak is sometimes called the "Stave Oak" because of its importance in the production of barrels and other wooden containers. It is also sometimes referred to as the "Champion Oak" because of its impressive size and age. The oldest known White Oak in the United States is the Wye Oak, which grew in Maryland and was estimated to be over 450 years old when it died in 2002.
