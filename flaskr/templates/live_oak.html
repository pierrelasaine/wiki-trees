Live Oak

History

Live Oaks (Quercus virginiana) are native to the southern United States and have been an important part of the region's history and culture for centuries. The trees were used by Native Americans for food, medicine, and building materials, and they played a significant role in the American Civil War as sources of shade and cover. Today, Live Oaks are a common sight in parks, neighborhoods, and other urban areas throughout the South.

Biology

Live Oaks are known for their twisting, sprawling branches and their thick, evergreen foliage. They can grow up to 80 feet tall and have a spread of up to 100 feet, with a wide, shady canopy. The bark of the Live Oak is deeply furrowed and gray-brown in color, and the leaves are leathery and dark green. Live Oaks produce acorns, which are an important food source for many animals, including deer, squirrels, and birds.

Fun Facts

The Live Oak is the state tree of Georgia and the official tree of both the city of New Orleans and the state of Texas. The oldest known Live Oak in the United States is the Big Tree in Goose Island State Park, Texas, which is estimated to be over 1,000 years old. Live Oaks are also featured in many works of art and literature, including the iconic novel To Kill a Mockingbird by Harper Lee.
