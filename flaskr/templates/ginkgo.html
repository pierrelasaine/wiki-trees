Ginkgo

History

Ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba) is a species of tree that is native to China, and has been an important part of Chinese culture for centuries. It was later introduced to other parts of the world, and is now grown as an ornamental tree throughout the United States. Today, Ginkgo is valued for its unique appearance, as well as its medicinal properties.

Biology

Ginkgo is a deciduous tree that typically grows to a height of 50 to 80 feet. It has a narrow, columnar shape and fan-shaped leaves that turn bright yellow in the fall. Ginkgo produces small, round nuts that are encased in a fleshy outer layer, and are a popular food source in many cultures.

Fun Facts

Ginkgo is sometimes called the "Maidenhair Tree" because of its fan-shaped leaves, which resemble the leaves of the Maidenhair Fern. It is also sometimes referred to as the "Living Fossil" because it is the only surviving species of its genus, and is considered a "living fossil" in the sense that it has survived relatively unchanged for millions of years. Ginkgo is also prized for its medicinal properties, and is often used as a natural remedy for a variety of ailments.
